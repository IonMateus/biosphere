About

Our project is an interactive web platform designed to enhance the exploration of biological research articles published by NASA. It tackles the challenge of navigating and connecting a large volume of scientific papers by transforming them into a dynamic visual network. Instead of long, static lists, researchers interact with a 2D canvas of interconnected articles, where each card represents a publication and each line shows conceptual similarity. This makes the exploration of complex scientific topics more intuitive, visual, and engaging.

Data and Methodology

The data foundation was built from a curated dataset of 607 biology-related articles, extracted in Python from XML sources. For each article, the code collected metadata such as titles, abstracts, authors, keywords, citations, and publication years, also calculating the most frequent and representative terms within the text. The result was a rich dataset with nearly 400,000 lines, later used to fuel the platform’s two main pages: Search and Insights.

Search and Insights

The Search page allows users to filter and visualize articles by keywords, authors, or publication periods, while the Insights page provides analytical views such as topic evolution, citation trends, and leading authors across time. Together, these features enable scientists to form a clear picture of the main research directions and relationships within their field.

Technical explanation of the correlation between cards

The connections between article cards are built in the buildSimilarityGraph function (file cards.js), through a multilayered affinity system:

  • Token similarity (65%) – Jaccard similarity of sets built from title, terms, and abstract openings.
  • Keyword overlap (≤35%) – Shared normalized key terms increase connection strength.
  • Author overlap (≤25%) – Matching surnames strengthen ties between related works.
  • Journal bonus (+0.12) – Articles from the same journal gain a constant affinity boost.
  • Temporal proximity (≤1) – Close publication years increase the connection score.

A dynamic threshold (around the 45th percentile) ensures adaptive sensitivity to dataset density, avoiding both excess and isolation of nodes. The resulting network produces thematic clusters that combine textual, authorial, and temporal coherence — enabling a structured yet organic map of scientific relations.

Impact and differentiation

The project’s main innovation lies in its visual and cognitive redefinition of how scientific knowledge can be explored. Traditional platforms present articles in long, linear lists — an approach that limits perception of relationships and broader patterns. Our system, on the other hand, spreads articles spatially across a 2D network, linking them visually according to similarity.

By integrating structured data, visual exploration, and analytical insights, the project significantly strengthens the process of identifying relevant, high-impact sources, making it a powerful tool for any researcher working with extensive academic literature.

Challenge

The NASA Space Apps Challenge asks participants to create innovative solutions for exploring and utilizing NASA’s extensive bioscience research to support human space exploration. The goal is to develop tools that help users navigate and understand the vast body of scientific publications related to biological experiments in space.

How we accomplish it

Our project reimagines how these research articles are explored by transforming them into an interactive, visual network. Through a dynamic 2D canvas, users can easily see connections between publications based on shared themes, authors, and topics, making complex research more accessible and actionable. This innovative approach leverages AI, data analytics, and intuitive visualization to support the discovery and exploration of key insights within NASA’s bioscience data.
